Pasta Dough

This is what I call my "base" pasta dough. I make this one the most because it's sturdy and flexibily meaning that it can rolled out very thin and not tear making it easy to handle. Its great for thin pasta or for ravioli's. You can adjust the recipe as much as you want, the rule of thumb is 1 egg for every 100 grams of flour.

If this recipe turns out well for you, look into variations with additions such as egg yolks, parsley, or other herbs and spices!

Ingredients

  • 400 grams all purpose flour
  • 4 whole eggs
  • 1/2 tablespoon olive oil
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • Water

Quick Instructions

  1. Measure out flour and salt in your bowl
  2. Make a well in the middle
  3. Add your eggs and olive oil into the well
  4. Using dough hook attachment, knead the dough
  5. Scrape down the sides of the bowl to fully encorporate the rest of the flour
  6. If needed, add a teaspoon of water
  7. Occassionally scrape out the bowl to help fully encorporate the flour
  8. Again, if needed add a teaspoon of water
  9. Knead the dough until all the flour is encorporated and dough a cohesive ball and matte on the surface
  10. Remove from bowl, press into a rough ball
  11. Cover tightly with plastic wrap
  12. Let it rest of room temprature for 40 minutes or in the fridge for some hours until needed (you can keep it for about a day)
  13. Portion the dough into 4 equal pieces, rewrap 3
  14. Lightly flour surface and top of dough ball
  15. Roll out into a rough square with a thickness of less than a finger
  16. Feed through the pasta machine on the widest setting twice
  17. Fold into a "packet" to square the dough
  18. Roll out into thickness of less than a finger
  19. Feed through the pasta machine on the widest setting twice
  20. Feed through the pasta machine on the next-lower setting twice
  21. Fold into a "packet" to square the dough
  22. Roll out into thickness of less than a finger
  23. Feed through the pasta machine on the widest setting twice
  24. Repeat, incrementing down the settings on the machine until you reach the desired thickness
  25. If at any point the shape goes astray, just refold into a packet and start again
  26. When you have your sheet, create the pasta shape you want!

Detailed Instructions

First lets address the elephant in the room, I will admit I use a stand mixer for kneading. You can easily do all the kneading yourself, but I'm a man of technology and will take advantage of one of my favorite kitchen gadgets.

Regardless, measure out your flour and salt into your bowl. Make a well in the middle with a spoon and add your eggs and olive oil. It's okay if if the eggs overflow (like mine did).

If you are using a stand mixer, put on your dough hook and stir on low so it mixes the middle portion together.

If you are using hands, stir the middle egg up with the flour, creating an ever-bigger well in the middle.

For those of you using hands, once you get to the outer edges, just fold the rest of the flour into the egg mixture. Give it a couple kneads in the bowl and turn out onto a surface and knead.

For the kitchen aid cooks, you will have to stop the mixer and use a spatula to scrape down the sides and bottom of the bowl to help the mixer along.

Whichever way you are kneading, depending on how big your eggs were or the humidity of your kitche, you may want to add a teaspoon or 2 of water if the dough seems a bit too dry.

Eventually, after about 5-10 minutes of kneading the dough will start to come together. Stand mixers: you may have to scrape the bowl down a time or two in order to help bring the dough together.

Eventually, the dough will become one cohesive mass and have a matte finish on the surface.

Roll out a large sheet of plastic wrap onto your work surface. Put your dough on it and form it into a ball.

Tightly wrap with the plastic wrap. We don't want the ball to dry out.

Let it rest at room temperature for about 40 minutes, or in the fridge for up to a day.

Meanwhile, get your work surface clean and equipment ready

After resting, unwrap you dough and cut into 4 equal pieces. I like to prevent the dough drying out, so I'll work with one half before cutting the other.

Flour your surface and the top of your dough ball. Roll it out until its less than the thickness of your finger

Set you pasta machine to the widest setting and pass the dough through. Do this twice

If at any point the pasta gets too "sticky", sprinkle some flour on top and wipe off the excess. Do this for both sides.

Your dough will look less like a blog and more of a really wide oval. This is what lamination is for. Fold the edges in and then the dough in half to make a "packet". This will help make the dough square shape the next time it goes through the machine.

Roll the dough out again, trying to keep a square shape. Pass through the machine again twice. Then change the setting to the next level smaller and pass the dough twice through this setting.

This time the dough will look alittle more rectangular. Repeat the lamination and now you might have a shape with corners!

If yours doesn't look so square, you can simply repeat the above steps. This can be true if at any time things go wrong.

Now again repeat above steps, but this time you can just stop the lamination and roll out the dough to the thickness you want. The dough is going to get very long as it gets thinner, so when handling it i will use the back of my hand to support it when feeding and grab it by the ugly ends.

If it gets too long, just cut the dough in half, cover with plastic wrap and continue on with the other half

When you are at your desired thickness, trim off the mishapen ends to square it. Now just shape the pasta into the shape you wanted!

I have a linguine die that fits on the back of the pasta machine.

After cutting I toss it with more flour to prevent the strands from sticking to each other and cover with pastic wrap.

The pasta I don't immediately eat will be frozen in nests and kept in the freezer for the future! You can also dry pasta on a cooling rack as well.

Enjoy!
